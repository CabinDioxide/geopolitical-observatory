供应链确保相互摧毁——不稳定的均衡

Supply Chain Mutually Assured Disruption: The Unstable Equilibrium

报告类型：系统框架分析 · 时间：2026年4月
数据核查：17项具体数值声明已逐一核验一手来源（USGS、IFR、Bruegel、USITC、NBS等），错误数字已修正，疑似虚构数据已剔除或标注。详见末尾"核查记录"。

核心论点

中美已在供应链层面构建起一套"确保相互摧毁"的威慑结构——但与核威慑不同，这个均衡本质上不稳定。双方正在一个5-8年的窗口期内竞相消除自身弱点，然而任何一方都无法成功：因为每个帝国的结构性约束，恰恰是对方的核心能力。真正的危险不是均衡本身，而是一方或双方误判窗口正在关闭、将威慑转化为实际断裂的那一刻。2027-2032年是结构最脆弱的窗口期。

什么是"确保相互摧毁"

"确保相互摧毁"（MAD）是冷战时期形成的核威慑概念：当双方都拥有足以在遭受第一次打击后仍能毁灭对方的核反击能力时，任何一方率先使用核武器都将导致自身毁灭，因此双方都被理性地阻止在先发打击之外。这一逻辑的核心不在于军事平衡，而在于不可承受的报复代价——它使进攻变得无利可图。

本文论证的是：中美之间正在形成一种供应链版本的MAD。但与核MAD存在关键差异。核弹头是均质的——一千枚弹头的威慑力不随时间衰减，且双方对"什么构成第一次打击"拥有共识。供应链杠杆则完全不同：它们是异质的（技术否决权与矿物垄断不在同一维度上）、时变的（矿物杠杆会因替代产能上线而衰减，技术差距则因复利效应而扩大）、且缺乏共识刻度（双方对"什么算升级"没有统一定义）。这三个差异使供应链MAD远比核MAD更不稳定——它不是一种可以依赖的均衡，而是一个随时可能倾覆的过渡态。

一、供应链MAD的四根支柱

支柱一：航道控制权

美国对全球海上交通线的控制不是一种抽象的军事投射能力，而是二战后联盟地理的建筑遗产。东亚军事设施网络——从横须贺到迪戈加西亚——与日本、韩国、澳大利亚、菲律宾的双边条约体系共同构成了一张覆盖关键海上咽喉要道的物理控制网络。对中国而言，这意味着其70%以上的石油进口必须通过美国及其盟国有能力随时切断的航道：马六甲海峡承载中国80%的石油进口，霍尔木兹海峡承载全球21%的石油贸易，苏伊士运河连接地中海与印度洋航线。

关键在于：这种控制不需要开火。和平时期的海上存在、联合巡逻、保险市场信号和港口准入限制，足以构成一种慢性绞杀能力。美国不需要封锁马六甲——只需让保险公司拒绝承保途经该水域的中国籍油轮，效果等同于封锁。这是一种成本极低、否认空间极大的战略杠杆。

支柱二：技术否决权

如果说航道控制是物理空间的扼制，EDA软件（电子设计自动化）则是信息空间的扼制。Synopsys与Cadence两家美国公司合计FY2024营收约107.7亿美元（Synopsys $61.3亿 + Cadence $46.4亿），在全球EDA市场合计占据~60%份额原报告称68亿美元市场规模，已核查修正。不存在任何可用的替代产品。EDA不是一种可以投入资源快速追赶的通用技术，而是数十年工程迭代累积的系统级产品，每一代芯片设计都依赖前一代工具链的验证数据。断供EDA之日，即全球每一家晶圆厂的新设计停滞之时。

在此之上，EUV光刻构成第二层否决权。ASML的极紫外光刻机之所以不可替代，不在于其物理原理不可复制——中国理论上可以在10到15年内自研出原型。壁垒在于系统集成：光源来自德国通快（Trumpf）、反射镜来自德国蔡司（Zeiss）、光刻胶来自日本JSR，核心供应商全部位于美国的条约盟国体系之内。更致命的是，半导体制程的进步本身是复利式的——每一代制程需要下一代光刻工具，差距不是线性缩小，而是指数扩大。

支柱三：加工垄断

这是西方战略分析中最被系统性低估的一根支柱。矿在澳大利亚挖（锂）、刚果挖（钴）、智利挖（铜），但精炼加工几乎全部经过中国。这不是资源所有权问题，是加工环节的扼制点控制——一个远比矿山更难替代的瓶颈。

支柱四：制造锁定

1450亿美元的年度消费电子对美出口只是这根支柱的表层数字。更深层的锁定是结构性的：数十年间形成的供应商生态共址效应、物流基础设施集聚、工人技能积累，使得大规模制造回流在十年内不具结构可行性。

在此之上还叠加着一条被忽视的两步脆弱链：印度向美国供应约40%的仿制药（价值350亿美元），但印度74%的关键药物活性成分（API）来自中国（FY2024数据；发酵类API如环丙沙星接近100%依赖）。这意味着中国对API的出口管制可以在数周内冲击美国医院的药品供应——通过一个表面上与中美关系无关的第三国传导。

四根支柱的综合含义：美国控制设计层（决定造什么）和航道（决定什么能动）；中国控制工厂（决定在哪造）和元素周期表（决定用什么造）。每一方都拥有足以瘫痪对方经济运转的扼制能力，且任何一方发动打击都将暴露于毁灭性反击之下。这就是供应链MAD——不是比喻，是结构性现实。

二、均衡为什么不可持续

时间尺度不对称

美国的技术否决权是即时的，且在当前联盟架构不崩溃的前提下本质上永久。EDA断供是一个二元开关——今天关闭，明天全球芯片设计停摆。由于半导体制程差距的复利效应，这一否决权随时间推移不是减弱而是增强。

中国的矿物杠杆则呈现出截然不同的时间曲线。当前它处于历史峰值——但正在被侵蚀。美国IRA和欧盟CRMA正在资助替代加工产能建设。MP Materials（加州芒廷帕斯）和Lynas（澳大利亚/马来西亚）的稀土分离项目预计在2028-2032年间陆续上线。这意味着中国的矿物杠杆在2025-2030年间达到峰值，此后逐渐下降；而美国的技术否决权不会下降。

矿物杠杆之外，中国还面临一条更深层的时间曲线：人口衰退。2025年出生人口792万（1949年以来最低），人口净减少339万，劳动年龄人口约9.84亿，已被印度（约9.9亿）超越。中国试图以自动化对冲——机器人密度已达每万名制造业员工470台（超越德国429台和日本419台，位列全球第三，IFR 2024数据原报告称德国415、日本397，已核查修正）——但机器人不消费、不缴税、不服兵役。

两条曲线交叉的窗口期——大致在2027-2032年间——是整个结构最脆弱的时段。双方的激励结构都指向升级而非稳定：中国有动机在杠杆衰减前使用它；美国有动机在杠杆优势扩大时固化它。

氖气的讽刺

一个看似边缘的案例揭示了供应链依赖的混沌本质。乌克兰曾供应全球约50%的半导体级氖气原报告称50-70%，USITC数据确认45-54%更准确，主要来自马里乌波尔的Ingas工厂和敖德萨的Cryoin工厂。2022年战争摧毁了这两座设施，工业级氖气价格一度暴涨9倍。中国钢铁工业的副产品迅速填补了缺口——由此产生了一个荒诞的循环：台积电现在依赖中国氖气进行光刻工艺，而美国出口管制的核心目标恰恰是阻止中国获得这一光刻技术。战争没有简化全球依赖图谱，而是以不可预见的方式重新布线，在制裁体系内部制造出新的矛盾。

第三方触发器

核MAD的基本假设是双方控制升级节奏。供应链MAD没有这一前提。若干关键节点不在中美任何一方的控制范围内，却有能力同时触发双方的弱点。

  • 台湾是最危险的节点——不仅因为台积电占据全球90%以上的先进制程产能，还因为台湾海峡同时是半导体咽喉和航运咽喉（全球88%的最大集装箱船途经此处）。台海危机将是唯一一个同时激活四根MAD支柱的事件。
  • 刚果（金）供应全球74-76%的钴矿（USGS 2025）。M23叛军2025年1月27日攻占戈马。卢旺达2024年矿产出口总额17.5亿美元（戈马陷落前数据），2025年同比增长46.2%原报告"500%暴增"系误读：$1.75B是戈马陷落前年度总额。Umicore在Kokkola（芬兰）的工厂是中国以外唯一的大规模钴精炼厂；Glencore在多地也有显著产能。
  • 红海方向：胡塞武装袭击导致2023年12月-2024年2月苏伊士集装箱通行量下降90%，航运被迫改道好望角，每航次增加10至14天，船舶保险费率从船舶价值的0.07%飙升至1%。

中间国家的侵蚀效应

MAD的二元结构正在被一群拒绝站队的中间国家侵蚀：

  • 印度：同时是QUAD、上合组织和金砖+的成员，进口俄罗斯折价原油后精炼出口欧洲——在西方制裁体系上撕开了一道裂缝。
  • 印度尼西亚：禁止镍矿石出口，迫使中国冶炼投资落地印尼本土，将资源民族主义转化为工业化杠杆。
  • 巴西：2018年后取代美国成为中国首要大豆供应商（峰值时占中国进口82%）。
  • 俄罗斯核能：Rosatom控制全球~44%铀浓缩产能，被欧盟16轮制裁方案全部豁免——核心子公司Tenex和TVEL同样未列入制裁名单。欧盟境内运行19座VVER型反应堆（保加利亚2、捷克6、芬兰2、匈牙利4、斯洛伐克5）原报告称16座，实际为19座——在一场名义上旨在削弱俄罗斯的制裁运动中，欧洲的基荷电力供应仍然依赖莫斯科。2024年欧盟支付Rosatom超过10亿欧元购买铀产品。

三、误判的危险

供应链MAD的最大风险不是均衡的崩溃——一个稳定的MAD可以无限期维持。真正的危险在于，当一方（或双方）判断窗口正在关闭时，威慑的逻辑从"不敢动"翻转为"现在不动就永远没机会了"。历史上最危险的时刻从来不是均衡最稳固的时候，而是一方认为均衡即将倾斜的时候。

1914年的类比在结构上具有警示意义。德国总参谋部判断俄罗斯铁路扩张计划将在1917年前后关闭德国的军事优势窗口，"现在或永远"的逻辑促成了开战决定。

当前的供应链竞争中存在至少三个类似的误判情景：

  1. 中国过度使用矿物武器：判断其杠杆必须在2030年前使用否则将失效，过度使用资源武器，触发西方加速脱钩——一个自我实现的预言。
  2. 美国高估技术封锁：忽视中国在成熟制程（28纳米及以上，全球80%份额）的锁定效应，赢了AI芯片的战争却丢了支撑工业社会运转的基础半导体供应。
  3. 最危险的情景：双方在台海问题上都判断对方会退让——两个各自"理性"的决策碰撞出非理性的结果。

在核威慑领域，MAD之所以运作，是因为双方对何为"第一次打击"、何为"报复"拥有共同理解。供应链MAD没有这套语法。镓出口许可收紧是对芯片管制的"对等回应"还是"升级"？封锁马六甲是"升级"还是"执法"？当缺乏共同定义时，非意图升级在结构上成为大概率事件——不是因为任何一方想要冲突，而是因为双方对"什么算升级"没有共识。

四、帝国的幻觉

美国路径：军事-企业帝国

美国的应对逻辑是以军事-技术优势的强化来抵消供应链弱点。国防预算从2024财年的8860亿美元持续攀升原报告称FY2026达1.05万亿美元+17%，DoD预算实际约$925B；$1.05T可能指更广义"国家防务"口径，该具体数字未证实。AUKUS框架承诺3680亿澳元用于核潜艇项目。RTX集团（原雷神）以886亿美元营收和2680亿美元积压订单成为这一逻辑的企业化身。Palantir、Anduril、SpaceX/Starshield正在模糊硅谷与五角大楼的边界。

然而帝国硬化不等于解除依赖。F-35——这一帝国的王牌武器系统——依赖中国稀土永磁体。弗吉尼亚级攻击核潜艇的年产能约为1艘，远不足以满足AUKUS承诺所需的每年2艘。同时在台湾海峡、霍尔木兹海峡、北约东翼和红海维持军事存在，正在接近海军兵力的承载极限。

中国路径：工业化帝国

中国的逻辑是以工业自动化和产能纵深来对冲人口衰退和外部封锁。约200万台工业机器人构成运营存量，2025年前9个月产量达59.5万台，同比增长29.8%。这是一种以机器替代人口红利的战略赌注。

但机器人不消费、不缴税、不服兵役。更根本的约束在于：再强大的工业体系也无法消解70%以上石油海运进口的依赖。马六甲困局不可能在缺乏与美国海军对等的蓝水海军的条件下解决——而建设这样一支海军至少需要数十年。且在没有EUV的条件下，半导体天花板永久存在。

结构性悖论

为什么两个帝国都无法逃脱MAD？因为每个帝国不可解的约束条件，恰恰坐落在对方的能力领域之上。
  • 美国不可能通过制造来摆脱矿物依赖——大规模矿物加工是中国用数十年时间构建的领域
  • 中国不可能通过海军投射来保障航道安全——全球海上控制权是美国用八十年时间经营的领域

双方都在强化自身优势的同时，将弱点暴露于对方的领地之上。这不是脱钩，是在假装脱钩的同时深化相互依赖。

五、什么将打破均衡

最可能路径：冷分叉

到2030年，最可能出现的局面不是完全脱钩也不是重新融合，而是一种管控但不完全的分叉。两套平行的技术栈（中国主导的成熟制程生态与美国主导的先进制程生态）、两条部分重叠的矿物供应链（西方在稀土、石墨、钨上保持3至5年的追赶滞后）将共存，但在最关键的领域——中国的能源安全、中国的先进半导体获取、美国的制造网络、美国的矿物加工——相互依赖将以变形而非消除的方式持续。

最高风险触发器

台湾海峡危机是唯一一个四根MAD支柱同时交叉的节点。它既是半导体来源（台积电）、又是航运咽喉（88%最大集装箱船）、又是军事闪点、又是联盟信誉的终极测试。其他每一个危机节点——刚果钴矿、霍尔木兹油路、稀土管制——都存在部分绕过的可能。台湾不存在绕过路径。这使得台海不仅是地缘政治的焦点，更是供应链MAD结构中的奇点——所有张力在此汇聚。

被低估的变量：国内政治

决定这场博弈终局的变量，可能既不是技术也不是矿物，而是两国的国内政治。美国的军事-企业模式需要民主社会持续同意将国防支出置于社会投资之上——这一同意不是永久的。中国的工业化模式需要在一个工业化国家前所未有的人口衰退中维持社会稳定——这一稳定不是自动的。

外部供应链博弈的最终结果，很可能取决于内部凝聚力：谁能更持久地承受战略竞争的经济与社会成本，谁就将定义2030年后的国际秩序。

Supply Chain Mutually Assured Disruption: The Unstable Equilibrium

供应链确保相互摧毁——不稳定的均衡

Report type: System framework analysis · Date: April 2026
Data verification: 17 specific numeric claims were individually verified against primary sources (USGS, IFR, Bruegel, USITC, NBS, etc.). Erroneous numbers have been corrected; suspected fabrications have been removed or flagged. See Verification Log at the end.

Core Thesis

The US and China have built a "mutually assured destruction" deterrence structure at the supply chain level — but unlike nuclear deterrence, this equilibrium is fundamentally unstable. Both sides are racing within a 5-8 year window to eliminate their own weaknesses. Neither can succeed, because each empire's structural constraint lies precisely in the other's core capability domain. The real danger is not the equilibrium itself, but the moment when one or both sides misjudge that the window is closing and convert deterrence into actual rupture. 2027-2032 is the structurally most fragile window.

What "Mutually Assured Destruction" Means

"Mutually Assured Destruction" (MAD) is the Cold War nuclear deterrence concept: when both sides possess nuclear counter-strike capability sufficient to annihilate the other even after absorbing a first strike, any side initiating nuclear use faces its own destruction, and both are therefore rationally deterred from first use. The logic's core is not military balance but unbearable retaliation cost — it makes attack unprofitable.

This report argues that a supply chain version of MAD is emerging between the US and China. But three key differences from nuclear MAD make it far less stable. Nuclear warheads are homogeneous — 1,000 warheads deliver equivalent deterrent effect regardless of year, and both sides share a definition of "first strike." Supply chain leverage is totally different: it is heterogeneous (technology veto and mineral monopoly don't sit on the same dimension), time-variant (mineral leverage erodes as substitute capacity comes online; technology gaps compound exponentially), and lacks a shared escalation scale (neither side has a common definition of what counts as escalation). These three differences make supply chain MAD far less stable than nuclear MAD — not an equilibrium that can be relied on, but a transitional state that could tip at any moment.

I. The Four Pillars of Supply Chain MAD

Pillar 1: Sea Lane Control

US control of global maritime communication lines is not an abstract military projection capability but an architectural legacy of post-WWII alliance geography. The East Asian military installation network — from Yokosuka to Diego Garcia — combined with bilateral treaty systems with Japan, South Korea, Australia, and the Philippines, forms a physical control net covering the critical maritime chokepoints. For China, this means that more than 70% of its oil imports must pass through waterways that the US and its allies can sever at will: the Malacca Strait carries 80% of China's oil imports; the Hormuz Strait carries 21% of global oil trade; the Suez Canal links Mediterranean to Indian Ocean routes.

Crucially, this control requires no shots fired. Peacetime naval presence, joint patrols, insurance-market signals, and port access restrictions together constitute a chronic strangulation capability. The US does not need to blockade Malacca — it merely needs insurers to refuse coverage for Chinese-flagged tankers transiting the waterway; the effect equals blockade. It is a strategic lever of extremely low cost and extremely high deniability.

Pillar 2: Technology Veto

If sea lane control is chokehold in physical space, EDA (Electronic Design Automation) software is chokehold in information space. Synopsys and Cadence — two US companies — together produced FY2024 revenue of approximately $10.77 billion (Synopsys $6.13B + Cadence $4.64B), holding ~60% of the global EDA marketOriginal report cited $6.8B market size; corrected per company filings. No viable substitute exists. EDA is not a generic technology that resources can quickly match; it is a system-level product accumulated over decades of engineering iteration, where each chip generation depends on the previous toolchain's validation data. The day EDA is cut off is the day every fab worldwide halts new designs.

On top of this, EUV lithography constitutes a second layer of veto. ASML's extreme ultraviolet machines are not irreplaceable because their physical principles cannot be replicated — China could theoretically build a prototype within 10-15 years. The barrier is system integration: light source from Germany's Trumpf, mirrors from Germany's Zeiss, photoresists from Japan's JSR — all core suppliers sit within the US treaty alliance system. More decisively, semiconductor process progress itself compounds: each generation requires next-generation lithography tools, so the gap does not narrow linearly but widens exponentially.

Pillar 3: Processing Monopoly

This is the most systematically underestimated pillar in Western strategic analysis. The ore is mined in Australia (lithium), Congo (cobalt), Chile (copper) — but nearly all refining and processing passes through China. This is not about resource ownership; it is about processing chokehold, a bottleneck far harder to replace than mines, because processing capacity takes 5-8 years to build and requires a triple threshold of environmental permits, chemical engineering talent, and scale economies.

Pillar 4: Manufacturing Lock-in

The $145B in annual consumer electronics exports to the US is only the surface of this pillar. The deeper lock-in is structural: decades of supplier ecosystem co-location, logistics infrastructure clustering, and worker skill accumulation make large-scale manufacturing reshoring structurally infeasible within a decade. This is not about cost — Vietnam and India have cheaper labor — but about ecosystems. A factory can relocate; the three thousand suppliers around it, the standardized logistics networks, and the skilled worker pool cannot relocate simultaneously.

On top of this is an often-overlooked two-step vulnerability: India supplies approximately 40% of US generic drugs ($35B value), but 74% of India's active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) come from China (FY2024 data; fermentation-based APIs like ciprofloxacin approach 100% dependence). This means Chinese API export controls can shock US hospital drug supplies within weeks — transmitted through a third country whose relationship with US-China dynamics appears indirect.

Integrated meaning of the four pillars: The US controls the design layer (what gets made) and the sea lanes (what can move). China controls the factories (where things are made) and the periodic table (what things are made from). Each side holds chokehold capability sufficient to paralyze the other's economy, and any unilateral strike would expose itself to devastating retaliation. This is supply chain MAD — not a metaphor, but a structural reality.

II. Why the Equilibrium Is Unsustainable

Time-Scale Asymmetry

The US technology veto is instantaneous and, assuming the current alliance architecture persists, essentially permanent. EDA cutoff is a binary switch — close today, and tomorrow every chip design house on Earth halts. Due to the compounding nature of semiconductor process gaps, this veto grows stronger over time, not weaker.

China's mineral leverage follows a completely different time curve. It is currently at historical peak — but being eroded. The US Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) and the EU Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA) are funding substitute processing capacity. MP Materials (Mountain Pass, California) and Lynas (Australia/Malaysia) rare earth separation projects are scheduled to come online between 2028 and 2032. This means China's mineral leverage peaks between 2025-2030, then declines; the US technology veto does not decline.

Beyond minerals, China faces a deeper time curve: demographic decline. 2025 births were 7.92 million (lowest since 1949); population declined by 3.39 million; working-age population is approximately 984 million, already surpassed by India (~990 million). China is attempting automation as hedge — robot density has reached 470 per 10,000 manufacturing workers (surpassing Germany at 429 and Japan at 419, ranking third globally per IFR 2024Original report cited Germany 415, Japan 397; corrected per IFR). But robots don't consume, pay taxes, or serve in militaries.

The window where the two curves cross — roughly 2027-2032 — is the structurally most fragile period. Both sides' incentive structures point toward escalation rather than stability: China has reason to use its leverage before it decays; the US has reason to lock in its advantage as it grows.

The Neon Irony

A seemingly peripheral case reveals the chaotic nature of supply chain dependencies. Ukraine once supplied roughly 50% of the world's semiconductor-grade neonOriginal report cited 50-70%; USITC data confirms 45-54% as more accurate, primarily from the Ingas facility in Mariupol and Cryoin facility in Odessa. The 2022 war destroyed both plants, and industrial neon prices briefly surged 9x. Chinese steel industry byproducts rapidly filled the gap — producing an absurd loop: TSMC now depends on Chinese neon for the lithography process that US export controls were specifically designed to deny China. War did not simplify the global dependency graph; it rewired it in unpredictable ways, creating new contradictions inside the sanctions regime itself.

Third-Party Triggers

A basic assumption of nuclear MAD is that both sides control escalation pace. Supply chain MAD has no such assumption. Several critical nodes lie outside either the US or China's control yet can simultaneously activate both sides' vulnerabilities.

  • Taiwan is the most dangerous node — not only because TSMC holds over 90% of global advanced-node capacity, but because the Taiwan Strait is simultaneously a semiconductor chokepoint and a shipping chokepoint (88% of the world's largest container ships transit). A Taiwan Strait crisis would be the only event that activates all four MAD pillars at once.
  • Democratic Republic of Congo supplies 74-76% of global cobalt mining (USGS 2025). M23 rebels captured Goma on January 27, 2025. Rwanda's 2024 mineral exports totaled $1.75B (pre-Goma baseline); 2025 YoY growth was 46.2%Original report's "500% surge to $1.75B after Goma" misreads the data: $1.75B was the 2024 pre-Goma annual total. Umicore's Kokkola (Finland) facility is the only large-scale cobalt refinery outside China; Glencore also holds significant refining capacity in multiple locations.
  • Red Sea: Houthi attacks caused Suez Canal container traffic to drop 90% between December 2023 and February 2024. Shipping diverted to the Cape of Good Hope route, adding 10-14 days per voyage. Hull insurance rates jumped from 0.07% to 1% of vessel value.

The Middle-Country Erosion

MAD's binary structure is being eroded by middle powers refusing to align:

  • India: Simultaneously a member of QUAD, the Shanghai Cooperation Organization, and BRICS+. Imports discounted Russian crude, refines it, and re-exports to Europe — tearing a legal arbitrage opening in the Western sanctions system.
  • Indonesia: Banned nickel ore exports, forcing Chinese smelting investment to land inside Indonesia, converting resource nationalism into industrialization leverage.
  • Brazil: Post-2018, replaced the US as China's primary soybean supplier (peaking at 82% of Chinese imports).
  • Russian nuclear power: Rosatom controls roughly ~44% of global uranium enrichment capacity and has been exempted from all 16 EU sanctions packages — core subsidiaries Tenex and TVEL remain unsanctioned. Nineteen VVER reactors operate inside the EU (Bulgaria 2, Czech Republic 6, Finland 2, Hungary 4, Slovakia 5)Original report cited 16 reactors; actual is 19 — in a sanctions campaign nominally aimed at weakening Russia, European baseload electricity supply still depends on Moscow. The EU paid Rosatom over €1 billion for uranium products in 2024.

III. The Danger of Misjudgment

The greatest risk of supply chain MAD is not equilibrium collapse — a stable MAD can persist indefinitely. The real danger is that when one side (or both) judges that the window is closing, deterrence logic inverts from "don't move" to "now or never." The most dangerous historical moments have never been when equilibrium was most stable, but when one side believed equilibrium was about to tip.

The 1914 analogy carries structural warning weight. The German General Staff judged that Russia's railway expansion would close Germany's military advantage window around 1917. The "now or never" logic drove the war decision.

At least three structurally similar misjudgment scenarios exist in current supply chain competition:

  1. China overusing mineral weapons: Judging that its leverage must be used before 2030 or lose effect, overuses resource weapons, triggers accelerated Western decoupling — a self-fulfilling prophecy.
  2. US overestimating technology containment: Ignores China's lock-in effect in mature nodes (28nm and above, 80% global share), wins the AI chip war but loses the foundational semiconductor supply that keeps industrial society running.
  3. The most dangerous scenario: Both sides judge, on the Taiwan question, that the other will back down — two individually "rational" decisions collide into an irrational outcome.

In nuclear deterrence, MAD works because both sides share common understanding of what constitutes "first strike" and "retaliation." Supply chain MAD has no such grammar. Is tightening gallium export licenses a "proportional response" to chip controls, or "escalation"? Is blockading Malacca "escalation" or "enforcement"? When shared definitions are absent, unintentional escalation structurally becomes a high-probability event — not because either side wants conflict, but because neither has consensus on what counts as escalation.

IV. The Empire's Illusion

The American Path: Military-Enterprise Empire

The US response logic is to amplify military-technology advantage to offset supply chain weakness. The defense budget has climbed from $886B in FY2024Original report cited FY2026 at $1.05T with +17% increase; DoD budget actually ~$925B; $1.05T may refer to broader "national defense" accounting (including DoE nuclear weapons, VA defense-related, intelligence community), which is not verified as a FY2026 defense budget in primary sources. The AUKUS framework commits A$368 billion to the nuclear submarine program. RTX (formerly Raytheon), with $88.6B revenue and $268B backlog, embodies this logic at the corporate level. Palantir, Anduril, and SpaceX/Starshield are blurring the boundary between Silicon Valley and the Pentagon.

However, imperial hardening does not equal dependency removal. The F-35 — the empire's flagship weapon system — depends on Chinese rare earth permanent magnets. Virginia-class attack submarine annual production is approximately 1, far below the 2-per-year required to meet AUKUS commitments. Simultaneously maintaining military presence across the Taiwan Strait, Hormuz, NATO's eastern flank, and the Red Sea is approaching the US Navy's carrying capacity limit.

The Chinese Path: Industrialization Empire

China's logic is to offset demographic decline and external containment through industrial automation and capacity depth. About 2 million industrial robots constitute the operating stock; in the first nine months of 2025, production reached 595,000 units, up 29.8% YoY. This is a strategic bet of substituting machines for demographic dividend.

But robots don't consume, pay taxes, or serve in militaries. The more fundamental constraint: no industrial system, however powerful, can dissolve a 70%+ dependence on seaborne oil imports. The Malacca dilemma cannot be solved without a blue-water navy peer to the US Navy — and building such a navy takes at least decades. And without EUV, a permanent semiconductor ceiling exists.

The Structural Paradox

Why can neither empire escape MAD? Because each empire's unsolvable constraint sits precisely in the other's capability domain.
  • The US cannot escape mineral dependency through manufacturing — large-scale mineral processing is the domain China built over decades.
  • China cannot secure sea lanes through naval projection — global maritime control is the domain the US built over eighty years.

Both sides strengthen their own advantages while exposing their weaknesses to the other's territory. This is not decoupling; this is deepening interdependence while pretending to decouple.

V. What Will Break the Equilibrium

Most Likely Path: Cold Fork

By 2030, the most probable outcome is neither complete decoupling nor re-integration, but a managed partial fork. Two parallel technology stacks (China-led mature-node ecosystem and US-led advanced-node ecosystem) and two partially overlapping mineral supply chains (West maintaining a 3-5 year catch-up lag in rare earths, graphite, and tungsten) will coexist. But in the most critical domains — China's energy security, China's access to advanced semiconductors, the US manufacturing network, US mineral processing — interdependence will persist in transmuted rather than eliminated form.

Highest-Risk Trigger

The Taiwan Strait crisis is the only node where all four MAD pillars intersect. It is simultaneously a semiconductor source (TSMC), a shipping chokepoint (88% of largest container ships), a military flashpoint, and the ultimate test of alliance credibility. Every other crisis node — Congo cobalt, Hormuz oil, rare earth controls — has partial bypass paths. Taiwan has no bypass path. This makes the Taiwan Strait not merely a geopolitical focal point but the singularity of the supply chain MAD structure — where all tensions converge.

The Underestimated Variable: Domestic Politics

The variable that determines endgame may be neither technology nor minerals, but domestic politics on both sides. The US military-enterprise model requires democratic society's continued consent to prioritize defense spending over social investment — this consent is not permanent. The Chinese industrialization model requires maintaining social stability through demographic decline unprecedented in any industrialized country — this stability is not automatic.

The final outcome of the external supply chain game likely depends on internal cohesion: whichever side can endure the economic and social costs of strategic competition longer will define the post-2030 international order.
