台湾供应链脆弱性评估（2026）

Taiwan Supply Chain Vulnerability Assessment (2026)

风险评分：92/100 — 极高脆弱 / EXTREME VULNERABILITY

核心论点

台湾是供应链MAD框架中的唯一奇点——全球唯一一个四根MAD支柱（航道、技术、加工、制造）同时交汇的节点。这一奇点给台湾带来历史上最大的议价权，但也使其陷入"悬链结构"的脆弱性——台积电的运营同时依赖四个不同国家（荷兰ASML、美国EDA、日本材料、德国光学）的持续合作，任何一条链断裂都会使整个结构失效。更深层的悖论：台湾的"硅盾"假设芯片垄断能威慑大陆入侵，但硅盾只在所有上游供应商同时合作时才有效——而这种合作可以因与两岸无关的事件（日韩争端、欧洲能源危机、美国政策突变）而破裂。台湾不是选择了奇点位置，也无法退出这个位置：一旦占据就不能放弃，一旦放弃就永久消失。

一、结构性画像：奇点的四维画像

台湾在全球生产网络中的位置不能用"半导体中心"这一单一标签描述。它同时占据四个维度的关键位置：

维度台湾位置结构含义

航运咽喉台湾海峡承载全球88%最大型集装箱船亚欧贸易的物理通道
军事闪点美中军事竞争的最高象征性焦点联盟信誉终极测试
联盟建筑美日韩菲安全架构的东亚锚点第一岛链的中心

这四个维度不是独立的——它们叠加在同一块领土上。台湾海峡的封锁同时切断：芯片出口、货物贸易、盟国投射路径、美国军事可信度。这是世界上没有其他地方同时具备的多维重叠。

四、结构性传导链：四位一体的奇点

传导链1：硅盾的悬链脆弱性

"硅盾"论点认为台积电的全球不可替代性威慑大陆入侵。本报告揭示其结构性缺陷——

台积电先进制程依赖： 荷兰ASML（EUV光刻） + 美国Synopsys/Cadence（EDA） + 日本JSR（EUV光刻胶） + 日本信越/SUMCO（硅晶圆） + 德国蔡司（光学） + 台湾高纯度水源（未被充分讨论的脆弱性） ↓ 上述任一国家的出口限制或供应中断 → 台积电先进制程停摆 ↓ "硅盾"丧失威慑价值——台湾的不可替代性建立在四个外国供应商的持续合作上，而非台湾自身能力

这意味着硅盾的维持不完全在台北的控制之下。2019年日韩半导体材料争端、2023年日荷加入对华芯片管制、可能的未来欧洲政策变化，都可以在不涉及两岸关系的情况下削弱硅盾。

传导链2：台海危机 → 四位一体崩塌

与其他国家不同，台海危机不是"一种情景"——它是四种情景同时发生：

大陆军事行动（封锁、围岛、或武装冲突） ↓ 同时激活： [1] TSMC停产 → 全球AI芯片$94B供应归零 [2] 台湾海峡航运切断 → 全球88%大型集装箱船改道好望角 [3] 西方军事响应 → 全球股市/商品市场崩盘 [4] 联盟信誉测试 → 日韩欧对华政策全面转向 ↓ 四条链的响应互相强化（不是独立加总而是乘积效应） ↓ 全球经济进入3-5年"冻结期"——比单一冲击严重一个数量级
奇点的定义特征：其他危机节点（刚果钴矿、霍尔木兹油路、稀土管制）都有部分绕过路径——可以用时间、成本、替代源吸收冲击。台海不存在任何绕过路径。这是为什么它在MAD框架中独占"奇点"地位。

传导链3："绕不开"的高纯度水资源

很少被讨论的台湾脆弱性：台积电需要极大量超纯水（单个先进制程晶圆厂每日用水15-20万吨）。2021年台湾大旱曾迫使台积电从水库购水和用卡车运水，生产险些中断。

气候变化 → 台湾降雨模式变化 ↓ 水库储水下降（台湾依赖台风季节降水） ↓ 工业/农业/民用水竞争 ↓ 如果无法维持水供应：台积电先进制程良率崩溃 ↓ (极端情景下)可能成为一个"自然原因"的硅盾失效——与大陆军事行动无关

五、时间变量：硅盾的"半衰期"

硅盾不是永久资产。其时间轨迹：

硅盾的悖论：台湾的战略最优策略是部分稀释硅盾——把不太敏感的制程（N3及以上）分散到Arizona、Kumamoto、Dresden，保持最前沿（N2及以下）在台湾。这有双重效果：(1) 降低全球对单一地理位置的风险敞口，让盟友不会在冲突中丢失全部芯片；(2) 但同时降低了"硅盾"的威慑力（盟友失去的越少，干预意愿越低）。这一悖论没有明确的"最优解"——台北的分散化策略是对两边都让步的务实平衡。

六、情景分析

情景A：大陆围岛演习升级为准封锁（2025-2030）

  • 触发条件：台独政治动作 + 美舰通过台湾海峡 + 大陆军演规模升级
  • 传导：航运保险费率飙升（~10x）→ 部分航运公司拒航 → 港口货物积压 → 台湾进出口部分瘫痪（芯片仍可飞运但大宗商品受阻）→ 持续1-3个月即引发台湾内部经济-政治危机
  • 落空条件：(i) 大陆政治判断转向缓和；(ii) 美国军事行动抢先介入改变态势；(iii) 台湾选举转向导致紧张缓和
  • 结构性影响：即使没有武装冲突，准封锁已足以展示台湾的供应链脆弱性，加速TSMC海外分散化

情景B：全面武装冲突（2027-2032高风险窗口）

  • 触发条件：见中国报告情景B
  • 传导（MAD全面激活）：所有4根支柱同时断裂——全球AI产业停摆、航运混乱、金融动荡、联盟全面重组
  • 落空条件：北京明确放弃武统选项 / 台湾内部政治转向亲大陆 / 美中达成长期"冻结"协议
  • 结构性影响：无论军事结果如何，台湾作为"硅盾节点"永久失效——要么被占领，要么经历如此规模的破坏以至于多年内无法恢复

情景C：上游供应中断（日本/荷兰政策变化，2026-2030）

  • 触发条件：日韩争端复发 / 荷兰政府更替 / 欧洲PFAS法规全面实施影响光刻胶
  • 传导：台积电某一关键输入供应中断 → 先进制程良率下降或停产 → "硅盾"被动失效 → 台湾议价权下降（盟友没有理由保卫一个无法运营的工厂）
  • 落空条件：上游供应商都维持对台出口（当前现状）
  • 结构性影响：这是台湾最不被讨论但可能性最高的情景——硅盾不是被军事力量打破，而是被看似无关的供应链事件掏空

七、结构性评估

"扭点主权"（Chokepoint Sovereignty）

台湾是供应链MAD框架中最极端的"扭点主权"案例——一个小经济体通过在全球生产网络中的单一不可替代功能让自己不可或缺。TSMC的制造能力是当代全球经济中最强大的扭点，超过ASML的光刻垄断（因为TSMC整合多项上游输入做成没有其他实体能以等量质量规模复制的成品）。

扭点主权的悖论

然而，台湾的扭点主权同时是其最大优势和生存脆弱性。"硅盾"假设台积电的不可替代性威慑大陆入侵，因为经济中断成本不可承受。但这一假设有一个被供应链地缘政治框架揭示的结构性缺陷：台积电的制造垄断依赖四个不同国家的持续合作，任何一国都能单独通过出口限制瘫痪台积电。台湾是芯片供应的上游控制者，但同时是芯片生产投入的下游依赖者——这种独特的脆弱主权只有在所有投入供应商同时合作时才有效。

揭示偏好：分散化是对自身脆弱性的明智回应

台湾的揭示偏好——把TSMC晶圆厂分散到Arizona、日本熊本、德国德累斯顿，同时保持最先进节点（N2以下）仅在台湾——显示台北理解地理集中的杠杆与脆弱性。把N2及以下独家保留在台湾同时分发较旧节点海外，是一项精密计算的策略：维持硅盾的威慑价值同时降低总集中的系统性风险。这是复杂的生产网络政策制定，但依赖一个稳定性假设（不发生台海危机）——这一假设越来越不确定。

与主题报告的交叉链接

  • 供应链MAD：台湾是MAD的唯一奇点——四根支柱同时交汇。其他任何危机节点（刚果钴、霍尔木兹）都有部分绕过路径，台湾不存在绕过路径
  • 半导体六层迷宫：台湾在第4层（晶圆代工）深度主导，在第5层（先进封装CoWoS）唯一规模化；但完全依赖第2层（日本材料）和第3层（荷兰ASML）的投入
  • AI物理约束：台湾是AI芯片供应链的起点，但CoWoS产能集中在台湾使"AI电力基建"讨论更复杂——美国数据中心即使解决了电力基础设施问题，若台积电停产，算力仍然不可得

Taiwan Supply Chain Vulnerability Assessment (2026)

台湾供应链脆弱性评估

Risk Score: 92/100 — EXTREME VULNERABILITY

Core Thesis

Taiwan is the singular singularity in the Supply Chain MAD framework — the only node globally where all four MAD pillars (sea lanes, technology, processing, manufacturing) simultaneously intersect. This singularity gives Taiwan the greatest bargaining position in history, but it also traps Taiwan in "suspension-chain vulnerability" — TSMC's operation simultaneously depends on continuous cooperation from four different countries (Netherlands ASML, US EDA, Japanese materials, German optics), and any single link breaking disables the entire structure. The deeper paradox: Taiwan's "silicon shield" assumes its chip monopoly deters mainland invasion, but the silicon shield works only when all upstream suppliers simultaneously cooperate — and that cooperation can rupture from events unrelated to cross-Strait relations (Japan-Korea disputes, European energy crises, abrupt US policy shifts). Taiwan did not choose singularity, and cannot exit from it: once occupied, cannot be relinquished; once relinquished, permanently lost.

Taiwan's position in the global production network cannot be captured by the single label "semiconductor center." It simultaneously occupies four dimensional key positions:

IV. Structural Transmission Chains: The Four-in-One Singularity

Chain 1: The Silicon Shield's Suspension Chain Vulnerability

The "silicon shield" argument holds that TSMC's global irreplaceability deters mainland invasion. This report reveals its structural flaw:

TSMC advanced-node depends on: Netherlands ASML (EUV lithography) + US Synopsys/Cadence (EDA) + Japan JSR (EUV photoresist) + Japan Shin-Etsu/SUMCO (silicon wafers) + Germany ZEISS (optics) + Taiwan high-purity water supply (underdiscussed vulnerability) ↓ Export restriction or supply disruption from any one of these → TSMC advanced-node halts ↓ "Silicon shield" loses deterrent value — Taiwan's irreplaceability rests on continued cooperation from four foreign suppliers, not on Taiwan's own capability

This means the maintenance of the silicon shield is not entirely within Taipei's control. The 2019 Japan-Korea materials dispute, 2023 Japan-Netherlands joining China chip controls, and possible future European policy shifts can all weaken the silicon shield without involving cross-Strait relations.

Chain 2: Taiwan Strait Crisis → Four-in-One Collapse

Unlike other countries, a Taiwan Strait crisis is not "one scenario" — it is four scenarios occurring simultaneously:

Mainland military action (blockade, encirclement, or armed conflict) ↓ Simultaneously activates: [1] TSMC halts → global AI chip $94B supply goes to zero [2] Taiwan Strait shipping cut → 88% of world's largest container ships reroute to Cape [3] Western military response → global equity/commodity market crashes [4] Alliance credibility test → Japan/Korea/EU policy toward China fully pivots ↓ Four chains' responses mutually reinforce (not independent summation but multiplicative) ↓ Global economy enters 3-5 year "freeze period" — order-of-magnitude worse than any single shock
Defining feature of the singularity: Every other crisis node (Congo cobalt, Hormuz oil, rare earth controls) has partial bypass paths — absorb shock through time, cost, substitute sources. Taiwan has no bypass path. This is why it occupies the "singularity" position in the MAD framework alone.

Chain 3: The Overlooked Water Dependency

Rarely discussed Taiwan vulnerability: TSMC requires massive ultra-pure water (a single advanced-node fab uses 150,000-200,000 tons of water daily). The 2021 Taiwan drought forced TSMC to buy water from reservoirs and truck it in, nearly disrupting production.

Climate change → Taiwan precipitation pattern shift ↓ Reservoir levels decline (Taiwan depends on typhoon-season rainfall) ↓ Industrial/agricultural/civil water competition ↓ If water supply cannot be sustained: TSMC advanced-node yield collapses ↓ (Extreme scenario) could become "natural-cause" silicon shield failure — unrelated to mainland military action

The silicon shield paradox: Taiwan's strategically optimal strategy is partially diluting the silicon shield — dispersing less-sensitive nodes (N3 and above) to Arizona, Kumamoto, Dresden, while keeping leading-edge (N2 and below) exclusively in Taiwan. This has dual effect: (1) reducing global exposure to a single geographic location, so allies do not lose all chips in a conflict; (2) but simultaneously reducing the "silicon shield" deterrent value (the less allies stand to lose, the lower their intervention willingness). This paradox has no clear "optimal solution" — Taipei's dispersal strategy is a pragmatic balance that gives ground to both sides.

VI. Scenarios

Scenario A: Mainland Encirclement Drills Escalating to Quasi-Blockade (2025-2030)

  • Trigger: Taiwan independence political moves + US vessels passing through Taiwan Strait + mainland drills scaling up
  • Transmission: Shipping insurance rates surge (~10x) → some shippers refuse routes → port cargo backlog → partial Taiwan import/export paralysis (chips still airfreightable but commodities impeded) → 1-3 months sustains economic-political crisis internally in Taiwan
  • Falsification conditions: (i) Mainland political judgment turns to de-escalation; (ii) US military action preemptively intervenes changing posture; (iii) Taiwan election turnover leads to détente
  • Structural consequences: Even without armed conflict, quasi-blockade is sufficient to demonstrate Taiwan's supply chain vulnerability, accelerating TSMC overseas dispersion

Scenario B: Full Armed Conflict (2027-2032 high-risk window)

  • Trigger: See China report Scenario B
  • Transmission (full MAD activation): All 4 pillars rupture simultaneously — global AI industry halts, shipping chaos, financial turmoil, full alliance restructuring
  • Falsification conditions: Beijing explicitly renounces military unification / Taiwan internal political shift to pro-mainland / US-China reach long-term "freeze" agreement
  • Structural consequences: Regardless of military outcome, Taiwan as "silicon shield node" permanently fails — either occupied or experiencing such scale of destruction that years to recover

Scenario C: Upstream Supply Disruption (Japan/Netherlands policy change, 2026-2030)

  • Trigger: Japan-Korea dispute recurrence / Netherlands government change / EU PFAS regulation full implementation affecting photoresists
  • Transmission: TSMC's one critical input supply disrupted → advanced-node yield drops or halts → silicon shield passively fails → Taiwan bargaining power drops (allies have less reason to defend an unviable facility)
  • Falsification conditions: All upstream suppliers maintain exports to Taiwan (status quo)
  • Structural consequences: This is Taiwan's least-discussed but highest-probability scenario — silicon shield is not broken by military force, but hollowed by seemingly unrelated supply chain events

VII. Structural Assessment

"Chokepoint Sovereignty"

Taiwan is the most extreme case of "chokepoint sovereignty" in the Supply Chain MAD framework — a small economy that makes itself indispensable to the entire global production network through concentration in a single irreplaceable function. TSMC's manufacturing capability is the most powerful chokepoint in the contemporary global economy, exceeding even ASML's lithography monopoly in systemic importance because TSMC integrates multiple upstream inputs into a finished product that no other entity can produce at equivalent quality and scale.

The Chokepoint Sovereignty Paradox

However, Taiwan's chokepoint sovereignty is paradoxically both its greatest strength and its existential vulnerability. The "silicon shield" thesis holds that Taiwan's semiconductor indispensability deters Chinese invasion because the economic cost of disruption would be unacceptable. But this thesis has a structural flaw the supply chain geopolitics framework reveals: TSMC's manufacturing monopoly depends on continuous access to inputs from four different countries, each of which could independently disable TSMC by restricting exports. Taiwan is an upstream controller of chip supply but a downstream dependent of chip production inputs — a uniquely vulnerable form of sovereignty that works only as long as all input suppliers cooperate simultaneously.

Revealed Preference: Dispersal as Sensible Response to Own Vulnerability

Taiwan's revealed preference — diversifying TSMC fab locations to Arizona, Japan's Kumamoto, and Germany's Dresden while maintaining the most advanced nodes (N2 and below) exclusively domestic — indicates that Taipei understands both the leverage and the vulnerability of geographic concentration. The decision to keep N2 and below exclusively in Taiwan while distributing older nodes abroad is a calculated strategy: maintain the silicon shield's deterrent value while reducing the systemic risk of total concentration. This is sophisticated production network statecraft, but it depends on a stability assumption (no Taiwan Strait crisis) that is increasingly uncertain.
