# Russia Supply Chain Vulnerability Assessment (2026)

## Risk Score: 29/100 — BALANCED

## Executive Summary

Russia's vulnerability score reflects an unusual structural position: massive export leverage in energy and defense systems combined with catastrophic infrastructure losses and market access restrictions from the Ukraine war. The destruction of Nord Stream pipelines and halting of European gas transit have permanently eliminated Russia's most valuable European energy leverage, while Western sanctions have severed access to advanced technology imports. Russia's pivot to China and India as replacement energy customers has preserved revenue flows but at reduced prices and increased strategic dependency on Beijing. Russia imports almost nothing from the assessed countries' dataset, making it structurally self-sufficient in crude terms but technologically isolated.

## Chokepoint Exposure

Russia's primary export routes have minimal maritime chokepoint exposure. Pipeline exports to China (Power of Siberia) and Turkey (TurkStream) bypass all major maritime chokepoints. Russian crude oil exports to India and China transit the **Strait of Malacca** (for some seaborne China-bound cargo) but primarily use direct Pacific (Kozmino terminal) and Indian Ocean routing. Russia's most significant "chokepoints" are not maritime but infrastructural: the destroyed Nord Stream pipelines represent permanent capacity loss of approximately 110 bcm/yr to Europe, and the halted Yamal-Europe pipeline eliminates another 33 bcm/yr. These are not chokepoints in the traditional sense but destroyed or denied infrastructure that cannot be rebuilt within this geopolitical cycle.

## Key Risks (2026 Context)

- **Permanent loss of European gas market**: Nord Stream destruction ($0B from former ~$40B/yr), Yamal-Europe halt, and Ukraine transit expiry collectively eliminate Russia's European gas leverage — Power of Siberia to China ($10B) and TurkStream to Turkey ($12B) cannot substitute for lost European revenue
- **Hormuz blockade benefits and risks**: Higher global oil prices benefit Russian crude revenue, but Russia's Iran partnership complicates diplomatic positioning; Russian crude discounts to India/China narrow as global prices rise
- **Technology sanctions**: Western export controls deny Russia access to advanced semiconductors, precision machinery, and aircraft components; Chinese substitution is partial and lower quality
- **Defense industry strain**: Ukraine war consumes military production capacity; defense export commitments to India ($7B) face delivery delays and quality issues
- **Shadow economy fragility**: Sanctions-circumventing supply chains through Central Asia and Turkey are subject to secondary sanctions enforcement and sudden disruption
- **Chinese dependency deepening**: Pivot to China for technology, consumer goods, and financial infrastructure creates new dependency that reverses the pre-2022 leverage relationship

## Structural Assessment (Chanyuan Politics Framework)

Russia represents the most dramatic case of production network sovereignty destruction in modern history. Before 2022, Russia occupied an enviable upstream position: controlling Europe's primary energy supply through a diversified pipeline network (Nord Stream 1/2, Yamal-Europe, Ukraine transit, TurkStream, Blue Stream) while maintaining defense export relationships across the developing world. This was genuine production network power — not the "resource curse" of a petrostate but structural leverage over the most advanced industrial economies on earth.

The Ukraine war and subsequent Western response destroyed this position with unprecedented speed. Nord Stream's physical destruction (September 2022) permanently eliminated the infrastructure of Russian energy leverage. European demand destruction through LNG substitution, nuclear restart, and renewables acceleration ensures that even if pipelines were rebuilt, the market has structurally shifted. Russia's revealed preference — invading Ukraine despite the certainty of sanctions — demonstrates that Moscow valued territorial and security objectives above production network sovereignty. This is a rare case where a state voluntarily surrendered its most valuable chanyuan asset for classical geopolitical objectives.

Russia's pivot to China creates a structural dependency reversal. Before 2022, Europe depended on Russian gas; now Russia depends on Chinese purchases of discounted crude, Chinese technology imports to replace Western sources, and Chinese financial infrastructure to bypass SWIFT sanctions. The Power of Siberia pipeline ($10B/yr) gives China leverage over Russia rather than the reverse — Beijing can negotiate price discounts from a supplier with no alternative market. In chanyuan terms, Russia has moved from "upstream energy controller of Europe" to "discounted resource supplier to China" — a catastrophic descent in the production network hierarchy.

## Recommendations

- **Maximize TurkStream leverage**: Turkey remains Russia's most valuable remaining energy customer in the Western orbit; expand TurkStream capacity and develop Turkish gas hub to access residual European demand via intermediary
- **Diversify Asian energy customers**: Expand crude exports to Indonesia, Vietnam, and Bangladesh to reduce dependency on China-India duopsony; develop Power of Siberia 2 pipeline only with pricing guarantees
- **Build domestic semiconductor capacity**: Current dependence on Chinese and gray-market chips is strategically untenable; invest in domestic fabrication capacity even at 28nm+ mature nodes for military and industrial applications
- **Stabilize defense export relationships**: Maintain India, Algeria, and Egypt defense partnerships despite Ukraine war production strain; these relationships are Russia's remaining non-energy production network assets
- **Develop Arctic shipping route**: Northern Sea Route offers sanctions-resistant export corridor to Asia that bypasses all chokepoints; invest in icebreaker fleet and port infrastructure along route
