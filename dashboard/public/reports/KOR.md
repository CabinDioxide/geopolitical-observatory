# South Korea Supply Chain Vulnerability Assessment (2026)

## Risk Score: 100/100 — NET VULNERABLE

## Executive Summary

South Korea shares the highest vulnerability score alongside Germany, driven by total energy import dependency, critical reliance on Chinese battery material processing, and exposure to five major maritime chokepoints simultaneously. Korea's position is uniquely precarious because its most valuable export — advanced memory chips — depends on upstream inputs from every major geopolitical fault line: Dutch lithography, Japanese chemicals, Chinese battery materials, and Persian Gulf energy. The 2026 Hormuz blockade compounds existing pressures from US-China tech decoupling, which places Korea's semiconductor industry in the crossfire of great power competition.

## Chokepoint Exposure

South Korea's supply chains transit five major chokepoints: **Strait of Hormuz** (Saudi crude $25B, Qatari LNG $14B — now blockaded), **Strait of Malacca** (Southeast Asian and Middle Eastern routing), **Taiwan Strait** (semiconductor component trade and regional shipping), **Suez Canal** (European and Middle Eastern goods), and **Bab el-Mandeb** (Red Sea energy and goods routing). This is the second-highest chokepoint exposure of any assessed country. The Hormuz blockade directly threatens $39B in annual energy imports with no pipeline alternative — Korea is an island economy in supply chain terms, entirely dependent on maritime shipping.

## Key Risks (2026 Context)

- **Hormuz blockade energy crisis**: $39B in Saudi crude and Qatari LNG directly disrupted; Korea's SPR covers ~90 days of oil but LNG storage is only ~2 weeks; industrial shutdown risk within 45 days of sustained blockade
- **Chinese battery material chokepoint**: Samsung SDI and LG Energy Solution depend on Chinese-processed cathode materials, cobalt, and lithium; any Chinese export restriction halts Korean EV battery production
- **US-China semiconductor crossfire**: Washington pressures Korea to restrict chip sales to China ($28B DRAM/NAND market); Beijing retaliates with battery material restrictions — Korea loses in both directions
- **ASML dependency**: Samsung Foundry's sub-3nm ambitions require continued ASML EUV access; any Dutch export control expansion would halt Samsung's leading-edge chip development
- **Japan semiconductor chemical leverage**: 2019 dispute precedent demonstrated Tokyo's willingness to restrict hydrogen fluoride and photoresist exports to Korea
- **Taiwan Strait scenario**: Korean semiconductor supply chain depends on TSMC for logic chip inputs and on Taiwan-strait shipping for broader trade; a conflict would sever multiple supply lines simultaneously

## Structural Assessment (Supply Chain Geopolitics Framework)

South Korea represents the most extreme case of "midstream vulnerability" in the supply chain geopolitics framework. Korean industry excels at a specific manufacturing tier — taking upstream inputs (lithography systems, chemicals, raw materials, energy) and converting them into world-class intermediate and final goods (memory chips, batteries, displays, ships). But Korea controls almost none of its inputs and has limited ability to dictate terms to its customers. The Samsung-SK Hynix HBM duopoly gives Korea genuine upstream leverage in one narrow domain (AI memory), but this leverage is dwarfed by the breadth of Korean dependencies.

The battery supply chain illustrates this structural trap precisely. Korea's three battery champions (LG, Samsung SDI, SK On) are globally competitive in cell manufacturing, but 70-80% of their cathode, anode, cobalt, and lithium inputs flow from Chinese processing facilities. Korea has attempted to diversify — direct lithium sourcing from Chile, cobalt from Congo — but the refining step remains Chinese-controlled. This is the supply chain geopolitics insight in action: mining the ore does not confer sovereignty if the refining is monopolized by a geopolitical competitor.

Korea's revealed preference has been strategic ambiguity — maintaining the US security alliance while preserving the China market ($28B in memory chip exports). But the tightening of US-China tech controls is collapsing the space for ambiguity. Korea is being forced to choose, and every choice costs: restricting China sales loses the largest market; defying US restrictions risks alliance fracture and technology access. This is the structural cost of midstream positioning without upstream control.

## Recommendations

- **Build domestic battery material processing**: Invest in lithium hydroxide refining, synthetic graphite production, and cobalt sulfate processing in Korea; accept 3-5 year cost premium to break Chinese processing dependency
- **Expand LNG diversification urgently**: Secure additional long-term US and Australian LNG contracts; build 4+ weeks of LNG storage capacity (currently ~2 weeks); develop floating storage and regasification units
- **Leverage HBM monopoly strategically**: Samsung and SK Hynix control 95%+ of HBM for AI — use this leverage to negotiate supply chain concessions from both US and Chinese customers
- **Reduce Japan chemical dependency**: Develop domestic photoresist and hydrogen fluoride production capacity; the 2019 dispute demonstrated that allied suppliers can be unreliable
- **Build strategic reserves for battery materials**: Stockpile 6-12 months of cathode precursors, cobalt sulfate, and lithium hydroxide as insurance against Chinese export controls
